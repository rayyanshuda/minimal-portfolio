The priest had said something about peace.
He remembered that. Not the words exactly, they had moved through him like water through a cracked vessel, leaving nothing behind except the shape of them. Peace. Rest. A better place. The congregation had wept in that collective, in an unashamed way that people weep when they are given permission to. His aunt’s shoulders shook. His uncle pressed a handkerchief to his face with both hands like he was trying to hold himself together physically.

He had stood at the graveside with dry eyes and empty hands and felt nothing except a vague shame at the nothing.

Someone had nudged him. 
*It’s okay to cry.*
He had nodded. He did not cry.

The pastor had bowed his head and invited them all to pray and he had stood there with his eyes open, staring at the mahogany box that held his mother, thinking; if there was something listening, it has terrible taste in what it chooses to take. He did not close his eyes. He did not pray. He decided then, quietly, and without ceremony, that nothing was listening. That nothing had ever been listening. That the universe was not cruel so much as it was simply indifferent, and indifference was somehow worse.

He went home and he drank.
_

That night he had been surrounded by casserole dishes and distant relatives and people who needed him to be okay so that they could be okay, so he had been okay. He had shaken hands and accepted condolences and said, “she was a wonderful woman” the appropriate number of times until the apartment emptied and the door closed and the silence arrived like a  guest that had been waiting outside all evening.

Then he drank.

Whiskey first because it was what she kept in the cabinet. “For guests,” she always said, though the guests were rare and the ullage in the bottle never seemed to change. And then whatever came after the whiskey, he couldn’t remember the order. He sat on the kitchen floor because at some point the chair had become too far away and the floor had seemed reasonable. The tiles were cold against his back and bottle warm in his hands.

He waited to cry. He had heard that alcohol loosened something in people, opened doors that propriety kept shut. He waited for the door to open.
It didn’t.

He waited to pray. Thought maybe the bottom of a bottle was where men found God, he had heard that too, seen it in films, the broken man on his knees reaching upward. He waited for the reaching.
It didn’t come.

He passed out on the kitchen floor with his mother’s whiskey, the last trace of her consumed, and woke up with the sun in his eyes, with a body that ached and a grief so dry it felt like dust and still, still, not a single tear.
He decided he simply wasn’t built for it. For crying. For praying. For any of the things that people did when the weight became too much. He would just carry it. He thought he was built for carrying.

He had been wrong about that.
_

The field had been beautiful once.
He knew this but it no longer mattered. The briefing had mentioned it. Sunflower field, long and endless plains, strategic position. Someone had even made a joke about it. Nassir, probably. Nassir made jokes about everything up until the moment he didn’t.
There were no sunflowers now.

The stalks that remained stood snapped and blackened, bent at angles that suggested violence rather than weather, their heads gone or crushed into the mud beneath boots and bodies and the weight of everything that had moved through here. The ones still standing looking less like flowers and more like witnesses, thin and scorched and unable to leave. The soil between them had been churned to a dark paste, turned over and over by the chaos until the earth itself looked wounded, gaping in places, collapsed in others, as though the ground had tried to swallow what had happened here and couldn’t finish the meal.

The sky above was a storm-dark colour, something that hadn't existed in the palette of normal skies he had seen. The smoke from what had burned earlier still moved through the air in slow, directionless columns, smearing the horizon into a bruise of orange and grey and a deep and sickly amber that caught the light wrong and threw it back wrong, so that everything below existed in a kind of false dusk, lit from no clear source, shadowed from no clear angle. 

And below it, the bodies.
They were everywhere. Scattered, force being its only architect. Men he had known by name, by voice, by the specific way each of them cursed when something went wrong, reduced now to shapes in the mud, to angles that living bodies don’t make, to the particular stillness that the eye recognizes before the mind catches up. Yuen, face down, one arm extended as though reaching for something just beyond her fingers. Dominguez on his back, eyes open, wearing an expression of mild surprise that death had not thought to change. Others he couldn’t name from this angle, from this position, from the ground where he had come to rest after the world had come apart. Someone’s helmet ten feet from anyone’s head. A rifle buried to the barrel in the earth as though planted there, intended to grow something from it. The smell was iron and smoke and something sweet underneath that he refused to think about.

The bone in his left leg had come through the skin below the knee, white and obscene against the torn red, existing outside the body only because it had been forced violently into the light. The flesh around it was ragged and dark at the edges, the wound gaping like a mouth mid-scream. Every breath pulled the pain tighter, a current so consuming it had stopped registering as pain and become instead a kind of white noise that his entire nervous system was broadcasting on every frequency simultaneously. 
He had tried to move it once in the first terrible minute after it happened. Just once. The sound that had come out of him was so primitive, living beneath language and reason and pride, something that only knew agony and the need to express it. He lay instead with his cheek against the wet earth, fingers working slowly through the mud in blind rhythmic pulls, clawing without purpose, without destination, not to escape but simply because the body had not yet received the message that escape is no longer the question.
His own warmth was leaving him. He could feel it going. Slow and patient, seeping out into the earth beneath him, and the understanding that his body had already begun the process of surrender without consulting him, that was the thing that cracked something open. Not the bone. Not the blood cooling against his skin. That.

And above him, the monster. 
It was massive, not just tall but dense, carrying a weight that the air around it seemed to feel, that the ground beneath its feet seemed to acknowledge with every step. It stood somewhere between eight and ten feet of pure and accumulated violence, the kind of size that stops being impressive and left him in denial of how such a body could exist. Its skin, if skin was even the right word for it, was the grey-black of iron left in fire too long, cracked in places along the joints and across the broad slabs of its chest where something darker showed through the fissures, not blood exactly, but something brewing underneath the surface wanting out. Old scars ran across in ridges so thick and layered they had become texture rather than injury, a topography of every wound it had ever been given and refused to die from.
Its armour, what remained of it anyways, was not worn so much as fused, plates of dark metal hammered or grown directly into the flesh at the shoulders and forearms, the edges jagged and unfinished, as though whoever had made it had stopped caring about craft somewhere in the middle and decided that function was enough. One pauldron was gone entirely, torn away or shed, leaving a shoulder bare that was wider than most men’s chests, the muscle beneath it moving with each breath, a witness that it was still alive after its recent injuries and scars. Its hands were enormous, the knuckles enlarged into something closer to weapons than joints, the fingers thick as handles, the nails, if they were nails, black and cracked and permanently filthy with something old. 

Its face was the worst part.
Not because it was monstrous, though it was, but because somewhere inside the monstrousness there were features. A jaw like a closed door, set and immovable. A brow so heavy it cast its own shadow over the eyes beneath it, deep-set and burning with a light that was nothing like fire’s warmth and everything like fire’s hunger. There was nothing behind them that could be reasoned with, nothing that could be appealed to, nothing that remembered mercy or had ever been introduced to it. There was a mouth somewhere below the jaw, barely visible, barely relevant, this was not a thing that needed to speak or eat or smile. The face existed only to aim the eyes and the eyes existed only to find the next thing that needed to die. Just rage in its oldest and most concentrated form. Just murder that had been given a body and ordered to walk. 
It looked at him.

*Please,* he said. Not to God. Not yet. To the monster. *Please.*
It didn’t move.
*Please.* His voice broke on the word, split open at the seam. *Please I’m.* He choked on this words. *Please just -*
He was weeping now. Not quite, not with any of the composure he had maintained at his mother’s graveside, not with any of the dry-eyed stoicism he had worn like armour for years. This was the door finally opening. Ugly and heaving and complete, the kind of weeping that empties a person, that takes everything that has been stored and compressed and refused and expels it all at once without asking permission. Tears and blood and mud mixing on his face and he didn’t care, couldn’t care, had no care left for anything except the word *please* which he kept saying because it was the only word he had left.
*Please don’t. Please. I’m still here. I’m still —*

The monster stepped on his leg. The sound he made, there are no words for the sound he made. It started below his stomach and tore itself upward through every chamber of him, stripping everything clean of its way out, and finally leaving his throat. Every nerve ending in his body testifying at once to a pain so enormous it had its own gravity. The bone shifted under the monster’s weight and he felt the grinding, the separation, the body communicating in frequencies that he couldn't explain. And his vision went white and then dark and then white again and his hands clawed so deep into the mud that the mud swallowed them to the wrist and still he screamed, still the sound continued, still his body insisted on expressing what his mind has already given trying to contain.

And then the monster lifted its foot. 
And he lay there. Shaking. Emptied. Something less than what he had been thirty seconds ago and he had not been much to begin with.
He wept. He wept with his whole body, shuddering, gasping, the tears coming so fast they stopped feeling like tears and started feeling like something structural giving way. He wept for Nassir and for Yuen and for Dominguez and for the others he hadn’t finished naming. He wept for his mother and for the kitchen floor and for the whiskey and for the dry eyes he had carried to her graveside like an insult. He wept for every door he had kept closed and every weight he had decided he was built to carry and every night he had sat in the dark and waited for something to open in him and felt nothing.
It was all opening now. All of it. All at once. And somewhere inside the opening, beneath the pain and the blood and the grief and the mud and the absolute and total ruin of everything, he found something he hadn’t been looking for. He pressed his back into the earth.

*Please.*
Not to the monster this time.
Please. I don’t know if you’re there. I never, I didn’t, I couldn’t —
He stopped. Started again.

*I stood at her grave and I couldn't pray and I don't know if that means you were never there or if it means I was never ready and I don't know which one is worse but I'm ready now. I'm ready because I have nothing left. I'm asking because there is nothing left to ask with except this.*
The monster shifted above him. He felt the ground tremble.
*Please don't let it end here. Not like this. Not in front of no one. Not before I —*
He didn't finish the sentence. He didn't know how. There were too many ways to end it and none of them felt like enough and maybe that was the point, maybe prayer was just the moment you ran out of endings and offered the open space to something larger than yourself and hoped it knew what to do with it.
He squeezed his eyes shut.

He kept praying.
The monster took one step closer.
He kept praying.
Another step. The ground trembled beneath it. The mud around him rippled outward from each footfall like the earth itself was flinching. He could feel the heat of it now, not warmth, nothing like warmth, a dry and ancient burning that had no interest in giving life to anything, only in unmaking it. The shadow it cast swallowed him completely.
He stopped praying.

Not because the fear had left him, it hadn’t, it was everywhere, it was in his teeth and fingertips and the hollow of his throat, but because somewhere between the screaming and the praying and the mud and the blood something had shifted. Something had loosened. Like a knot that had been pulled tight for so long that the rope had forgotten it was ever straight, and now, slowly, it was releasing. Not because God had answered. Not because the fear had left. But because somewhere between the last word and the silence that followed it, he realized he had already said everything. That there was nothing left to say. That the prayer had not been for rescue, it had been for release. And the release had already happened. Somewhere between the screaming and the weeping and the pressing of his body into the blood-soaked earth, the knot had come undone.
He had been carrying it for so long.

He looked up at the monster.
He didn't look away.
I'm sorry, he whispered. Not to the monster. Not even to God. To her. To Nassir and Yuen and Dominguez and the others whose names he hadn't finished grieving. To every version of himself that had sat in the dark and decided nothing was listening and closed every door and carried every weight alone and called it strength.
*I'm sorry I held it all so tight.*

It hadn't moved. It stood above him with the patience of something that had never needed to hurry, that had never known urgency because urgency implies the possibility of failure and failure was not a language this creature spoke. The smoke moved through the air behind it in slow orange strands. The false dusk light caught the edges of its fused armour and threw them back in dull and ugly gleams. Its breathing came and went like tides on a shore that no one visited.

He met its eyes.
He didn't look away.
There was something almost clarifying about it, about lying broken in the mud beneath a thing that intended to end him and simply looking at it. No more running. No more carrying. No more pretending. He was here and it was here and the field was full of the men who had suffered the same fate he was going to, and his leg was destroyed and his God, if there was one, if there had ever been one, if the prayer had reached anything at all, was silent.

He thought about his mother.
He thought about her face, the way she used to look at him when he was small and the world was large and she was the thing between him and all of it. The specific warmth of it, her eyes on him like he was something worth looking at. Like he was something the world had gotten right. He hadn't thought about that face in years. He had been too busy carrying everything else to go looking for it. She was clearer now than she had been even in life. That specific certainty that he was seen and known and not found wanting. He found it now, at the bottom of everything, where the carrying had finally stopped.

A strange and terrible peace moved through him then. The feeling of a man who has run out of resistance and discovered that the resistance was the heaviest thing he was carrying. The feeling of open hands after years of fists.

He lay in the mud and he looked up and he let go.
The monster raised its foot.
It moved slowly. The foot rose above him and blocked out what remained of the sky and for a moment he was in its shadow entirely, in a darkness that was total despite the burning eyes above him, and the shadow was cold in a way that had nothing to do with temperature.
He didn't close his eyes.
He had spent too long with his eyes closed.

He had never understood people who said they weren't afraid of death.
He had always assumed it was performance, the brave face that people put on when the alternative was admitting something too large and too final to look at directly. He had said it himself once or twice, in the casual manner of young men saying things they haven't earned yet. *I'm not afraid of dying.* Said it like a fact. Like something he had tested and confirmed.
He understood now that he had never been anywhere near it before.
But something had changed in the last few minutes. In the space between the praying and the silence and the warmth that had no right to be there. Something had rearranged itself inside him, not dramatically, not with the sweeping grandeur of a man arriving at peace in a film, but quietly, like rooms that look different in the morning than they did the night before, same furniture, same walls, but the light coming from a different angle and making everything mean something slightly different than it did before.
He was not afraid.
He was so tired.

The foot came down again, on his chest this time. The monster’s weight was absolute. He could feel the earth beneath him giving slightly, receiving him, without preference, without ceremony, without the faintest acknowledgement that what it was taking had ever been anything other than temporary.
His chest could not rise.
He discovered this with a strange curiosity. His lungs asked for air and the air was not available and the asking grew more urgent and then less urgent and then quiet. The asking had not been answered, it had been gently and permanently retired.
His hands had stopped moving in the mud.
He hadn’t noticed when. They lay open now, palms up, fingers loose. There was something about that detail that would have meant something to him if he’d had the remaining capacity to think about what things meant. Open hands. After all the fists. 

The red behind his eyes was softening.
He had expected darkness at the end of things, sudden, absolute, final, a light switch snapping down with a sharp click. Instead it was more like a dusk. More like the sky above the field had descended and made a home inside him, that bruised and amber dissolution, that gradual and almost tender erasure of edges and boundaries and the hard lines that separate one thing from another.

He was becoming less defined.
He could feel it, the borders of himself growing uncertain, the precise coordinates of where he ended and everything else began losing their authority, their conviction, their insistence on being exactly where they were. He had spent his whole life being very precisely himself, very carefully contained, and now the boundaries were simply leaving, one by one, like guests at the end of a long night who had stayed as long as they were going to stay.

He thought about the warmth.
It was still there. Impossibly, stubbornly, with no regard for the circumstances or the physics or the theology of a man who had spent thirty years deciding nothing was listening, it was still there. Small and certain in the center of him. Not growing, not speaking, not performing any of the miracles he had been told God was in the business of performing. Just present. Just warm.

He had not felt that in thirty years.
He felt it now.
There you are, he thought. Or maybe he didn't think it, maybe it simply moved through him, without origin, belonging equally to everywhere and nowhere.

*There you are.*
The red was almost gone.
He was no longer in pain.
His leg was gone, or rather the leg's insistence on being felt was gone, had dissolved into the larger dissolving, had joined the borders and the boundaries and the carried weights and the closed doors in whatever was releasing itself from him in these last and quiet moments.
The monster above him was still breathing.

He almost wanted to thank it.
He didn't.
But almost.
His last breath left him without announcement. No rattle, no gasp, none of the theatrical scene of death in stories. Just an exhale that went slightly longer than the one before it, that kept going past the point where an inhale should have followed, that continued until it had given back everything it had been holding and then simply,
Stopped.
His eyes remained open.
They looked up through the monster, through the smoke and the amber sky and the burnt-coloured light, through everything that the world had put between him and whatever was above it, with an expression that the men who would find him the following morning would struggle to describe in their reports. Not peace exactly. Not happiness. Not the slack absence of expression that death usually leaves behind.
Something that looked, irrationally, with no explanation that any of them would be able to offer.
Like a man who had finally put something down.

But what they wouldn't be able to write in their reports was this:
That in his last moments he had wept without audience. That he had broken without performance. That he had said sorry to people who could not hear him and meant every word of it. That he had prayed for the first time in his life with mud on his face and blood on his hands and no dignity left to protect, and that it had been the most honest thing he had ever done.
They would write: soldier. They would write: deceased. They would write whatever words fit the boxes on the form.
They would not write that he had spent thirty years carrying everything alone and had finally, in the last place and the last moment available to him, set it all down.
They would not write that he had gone out the way he had never let himself live.

Fully open.
Completely human.
